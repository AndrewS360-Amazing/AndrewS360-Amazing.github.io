The City That Never Sleeps

New York City never stops. We never slow down. We have the reputation for being called “The City that Never Sleeps” and we all represent it. Our lights stay on through the rain, the fog, and the long overtime nights. New York is not defined by pace, but by its refusal to wait for anyone. It is the speed that defines New York, the impatience that exhausts us, but also empowers us. We, as New Yorkers, are constantly moving around. Living in New York City is challenging because of its fast-paced, chaotic, and selfish environment but this culture has shaped New Yorkers to be resilient, resourceful, and persevering to ensure our survival.

The drive and motivation, both tiresome and rewarding, is the essence of being a New Yorker. New Yorkers are always rushed; in fact, the average New Yorker wakes up at 6am in a hurry, flies through their morning routine in under 15 minutes and zooms to the train in five minutes. However, the train would close its doors and depart at 6:20 and if you did your math correctly, the average New Yorker would get to the train at the instant it closes its doors. Then, we would jam the train door with our arm like getting to work or school or anywhere you have to go is more important than your arm. You jam the door with your arm not only because you need to get to work on time, but it is also how the city says, You’re in or you’re out. The train, of course, would let you on, but everybody else would complain at you for holding everybody up. But you don’t care because you can do whatever you want. In reality, although everybody is complaining about you holding back the train, they are more worried about themselves than you. See, nobody in New York cares for each other. They are all caring about, “can I get to work on time?” and if the answer is no, the first thought they have is, “I gotta wake up earlier and plan for these situations.” Nobody really blames you for making them late because if they did, then they are not surviving. In New York, everybody knows that you cannot blame others for your problems. You being late is your problem and not mine so all you can do is do better next time.

If you ever go down to the financial district in downtown Manhattan, take a note of the people. Most people there are walking fast, with their heads up high, full of confidence, and their hands swinging side by side rhythmically. Somehow, with the help of luck, we can walk extremely fast and never bump into a single person. However, sometimes we do and there are two types of people who would respond. People who are mentally ill and would take it so personally like you’re a criminal or something and start yelling at you and by that time, they can just move to Jersey and terrorize other parts of the country, and then there is the person who looks at you with a smile and says, “It’s ok” and you go on with your day. New Yorkers just look nice and say nice things to you just to ignore you. It is because nobody really wants to have a situation on the street. Everybody wants to get through their day and go home, so in reality, we may seem kind, but we are really sensitive to that “bump on the street.” Cars and taxis rush down downtown Manhattan and one time, while crossing the street, like a pro-New Yorker, I ran across the street at the last second when the streetlight flipped from red to green. The taxi honked at me and stuck up the middle finger in front of me. New Yorkers are very rude and mean because we only care about our own priorities.

The constant momentum of New York, defined by the ability to finish one task and jump to the next, is one of the city’s greatest contradictions because that rarely happens without disruptions. There are always inconveniences and it is how we adapt to them that allows us to survive. Living in New York means being constantly tested by challenges. New York demands resilience, whether it is a train stalled between stations saying, “There is a delay at West 4th Street Washington Square,” or when prices and rent are soaring faster than paychecks.

Living in this city requires patience and perseverance to succeed. These tests forge a quiet pride, the belief that surviving in New York is an accomplishment in itself. However, this endurance can harden into indifference. The same strength that keeps New Yorkers thriving and being driven towards their goals is the same strength that isolates ourselves. On the train, we learn to look and ignore the homeless person sleeping on the platform. Most of us would walk around them and never give them anything because it is our survival instinct. In New York, surviving is the main priority over pity or sympathy for others. The constant pressure of “Can I provide for my family,” and “Can I pay rent this month,” makes us focus more on our own struggles and causes us to ignore others around us. Yes, we see them suffering, but after all, survival is the city’s most contagious habit.

So when people say New York never sleeps, they miss the point. The City never sleeps but it also dreams of success the next day. The dreams carried by its people through ambition, struggle, chaos, and the unrelenting will to move forward. And when the next day comes, those dreams are executed with millions of hurried footsteps, echoing down avenues and streets, refusing to wait for anyone or anything. And through the rhythm of the fast-paced environment, we learn that living in New York is tough and gritful, but extremely gratifying in the end.
